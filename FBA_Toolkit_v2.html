What's Inside This Toolkit

Developed from original thesis research identifying gaps in pre-service SNEd teacher knowledge of FBA. All resources are grounded in evidence-based practice and Philippine inclusive education policy.

About This Research

This toolkit is the primary output of the study "Exploring the Level of Awareness of Pre-Service Special Needs Education Teachers on Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA): A Basis for FBA Workshop." The research identified significant gaps in pre-service teacher knowledge of FBA concepts, data collection techniques, and intervention planning — gaps this workshop and toolkit are designed to address.

The study employed quantitative and qualitative methods, including a researcher-made survey instrument and Focus Group Discussions (FGDs) with 10 pre-service BSNEd teachers, yielding nine thematic findings that directly shaped every component of this toolkit.

Evidence-Based Practice

Practical Skills for FBA Implementation

Research identified that pre-service teachers struggle most with writing operationally defined, measurable behavior descriptions and translating FBA findings into effective BIPs. These exercises directly target those gaps.

Skill 1: Defining Behaviors in Observable and Measurable Terms

A critical first step in any FBA is operationally defining the target behavior — describing it in objective, observable, and measurable language that any observer can reliably identify and record. Vague language (e.g., "aggressive," "non-compliant") must be replaced with precise behavioral descriptions (Cooper et al., 2020).

  • Step 1 — Identify the behavior of concern. Write down the behavior in plain language (e.g., "hits classmates," "refuses tasks").
  • Step 2 — Apply the Dead Man's Test. If a dead person could do it (e.g., "sits quietly"), it is not a behavior — revise so it describes active, observable action.
  • Step 3 — Include topography, not interpretation. Describe what the behavior looks, sounds, or feels like without inferred motivation. Instead of "gets angry," write "raises voice above 75 dB, pushes materials off desk."
  • Step 4 — Ensure measurability. The definition should enable consistent frequency, duration, or intensity measurement across observers.
  • Step 5 — Pilot-test inter-observer agreement. Two observers independently record the behavior using your definition. Aim for ≥80% agreement (Cooper et al., 2020).

Practice Exercise

Read the vague description below, then rewrite it as an operational definition in the field provided.

Vague: "Carlos is aggressive during group work."

Model Answer:

During group work activities lasting more than 5 minutes, Carlos physically strikes peers with an open or closed hand at least once per session (frequency count), and verbally shouts at volume audible across the classroom (duration recording).

Cooper, J. O., Heron, T. E., & Heward, W. L. (2020). Applied behavior analysis (3rd ed.). Pearson. | IRIS Center. (2024). FBA (elementary): Identifying the reasons for student behavior. Vanderbilt University. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/fba-elem/cresource/q2/p03/#content

Skill 2: Conducting Indirect Assessments (Interviews & Rating Scales)

Indirect assessments gather information about a behavior from people who know the student well — teachers, parents, and the student themselves. These tools help generate initial hypotheses before direct observation begins (O'Neill et al., 2015).

  • Step 1 — Select the appropriate tool. Common indirect assessment tools include the Functional Assessment Interview (FAI), the Motivation Assessment Scale (MAS), and structured teacher rating forms.
  • Step 2 — Interview key informants. Ask about setting events, antecedents, behavior topography, maintaining consequences, and previous interventions.
  • Step 3 — Triangulate data across informants. Compare responses from teachers, parents, and (where appropriate) the student to identify consistent patterns.
  • Step 4 — Generate a preliminary hypothesis. Based on interview data, draft a tentative function (attention, escape, sensory, tangible) before moving to direct observation.

O'Neill, R. E., Albin, R. W., Storey, K., Horner, R. H., & Sprague, J. R. (2015). Functional assessment and program development (3rd ed.). Cengage. | IRIS Center. (2024). Indirect assessments. Vanderbilt University. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/fba-elem/cresource/q2/p05/#content

Skill 3: Conducting Descriptive Assessments (ABC Direct Observation)

Descriptive assessments involve direct, systematic observation of the student in natural settings. Observers record antecedents, behaviors, and consequences in real time using structured forms, allowing identification of conditional probabilities (patterns between triggers and outcomes) (Umbreit et al., 2007).

  • Step 1 — Prepare your ABC form. Use the standardized form in the ABC & BIP Forms section of this toolkit. Ensure the target behavior is already operationally defined (see Skill 1).
  • Step 2 — Conduct unobtrusive observation. Observe across multiple settings and times of day (minimum 3–5 sessions) to capture the full behavioral pattern.
  • Step 3 — Record with specificity. Document exact antecedents (not interpretations), precise topography of behavior, and actual consequences — not assumed ones.
  • Step 4 — Analyze for patterns. Identify recurring antecedents and consistent consequences across observations to determine the maintaining function.
  • Step 5 — Calculate conditional probabilities. What percentage of the time does consequence X follow behavior Y? Higher percentages suggest a functionally related consequence.

Umbreit, J., Ferro, J., Liaupsin, C., & Lane, K. (2007). Functional behavioral assessment and function-based intervention. Pearson. | IRIS Center. (2024). Descriptive assessments. Vanderbilt University. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/fba-elem/cresource/q2/p06/#content

Skill 4: Writing Hypothesis Statements

A hypothesis statement integrates FBA data into a testable prediction that links antecedents, the target behavior, and its maintaining function. It is the foundation upon which the BIP is built (Crone et al., 2015).

  • Step 1 — Use the three-part format. A complete hypothesis states: (1) the triggering antecedent condition, (2) the operationally defined target behavior, and (3) the maintaining consequence/function.
  • Step 2 — Template. "When [antecedent/setting event], [student name] engages in [target behavior defined in measurable terms] in order to [function: obtain/escape/avoid/communicate]."
  • Step 3 — Ground it in data. Every part of the hypothesis must be directly supported by your ABC observations — not assumptions.
  • Step 4 — Verify with a structural or functional analysis if needed. Systematic manipulation of conditions can confirm the hypothesized function before BIP development.

Practice Exercise

Using the ABC data below, write a complete hypothesis statement.

ABC Summary: During independent seatwork, Ana repeatedly puts her head on the desk and covers her ears (3 out of 4 sessions observed). Each time, the teacher removes the worksheet and offers a sensory break.

Model Answer:

When presented with independent written seatwork in a busy classroom, Ana places her head on the desk and covers her ears (observed during 75% of seatwork periods) in order to escape/avoid the demanding academic task and/or overwhelming sensory stimulation.

Crone, D. A., Hawken, L. S., & Horner, R. H. (2015). Building positive behavior support systems in schools (2nd ed.). Guilford Press. | IRIS Center. (2024). Hypothesis statements. Vanderbilt University. https://iris.peabody.vanderbilt.edu/module/fba-elem/cresource/q2/p07/#content

Skill 5: Developing a Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP)

A BIP translates the FBA hypothesis into a structured, individualized plan that addresses both the function of the behavior and the instruction of a socially appropriate replacement behavior. Effective BIPs are function-based and include antecedent modifications, skill instruction, and consequence strategies (Crone et al., 2015).

  • Step 1 — Confirm behavioral function. Revisit the FBA hypothesis. Every BIP element must align with the confirmed function (attention, escape, sensory, or tangible).
  • Step 2 — Identify a functionally equivalent replacement behavior (FERB). The replacement behavior must access the same function as the problem behavior — only more efficiently and socially acceptably (e.g., requesting a break card instead of throwing materials to escape a task).
  • Step 3 — Plan antecedent modifications. Alter conditions that trigger the behavior: modify task difficulty, adjust seating, provide advance warning of transitions, or incorporate student preference.
  • Step 4 — Plan reinforcement for the FERB. The replacement behavior must be reinforced more richly and immediately than the problem behavior to ensure it "competes" successfully.
  • Step 5 — Plan the response to the problem behavior. Specify how the team will respond when the target behavior occurs — typically extinction (withholding the maintaining consequence) plus safety protocols if needed.
  • Step 6 — Design a progress monitoring system. Identify how and how often behavioral data will be collected to evaluate whether the BIP is working.
  • Step 7 — Specify roles and review dates. Assign responsibilities to each team member and set a formal review date (typically 4–6 weeks after implementation).

Glossary of FBA Terms

Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA)
A systematic process for identifying the antecedents, consequences, and function of a challenging behavior in order to develop effective, individualized behavior support plans (Cooper et al., 2020).
ABC Model
A three-component framework — Antecedent (what happens before), Behavior (observable action), and Consequence (what happens after) — used to identify behavioral patterns and maintaining functions (Skinner, 1953, as cited in Cooper et al., 2020).
Operational Definition
A precise, observable, and measurable description of a behavior that enables consistent identification and recording across multiple observers (Cooper et al., 2020).
Hypothesis Statement
A data-based prediction that links a triggering antecedent condition, the operationally defined target behavior, and the maintaining consequence or behavioral function (Crone et al., 2015).
Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP)
A written, individualized plan derived from FBA findings that specifies antecedent modifications, replacement behavior instruction, reinforcement strategies, and progress monitoring procedures (IRIS Center, 2024).
Replacement Behavior
A socially appropriate behavior that serves the same function as the problem behavior and is therefore more efficient for accessing the desired consequence (Horner & Day, 1991, as cited in Cooper et al., 2020).
Function of Behavior
The maintaining consequence that reinforces a behavior — most commonly categorized as attention, escape/avoidance, access to tangibles, or sensory stimulation (O'Neill et al., 2015).

Practice Scenarios

FBA Case Studies

Four evidence-based scenarios for hands-on practice with ABC data analysis and hypothesis formation. Expand each case to review full details.

A — Antecedent

Teacher gives John a math worksheet and asks him to complete it independently.

B — Behavior

John crumples the paper, throws it on the floor, and loudly says "I don't want to!"

C — Consequence

Teacher removes the worksheet and allows John to sit quietly until the next activity.

Hypothesis Statement: When presented with independent written academic tasks, John engages in disruptive behavior (throwing materials, verbal refusal) in order to escape/avoid the difficult task.

Intervention Idea: Provide modified worksheets with fewer problems; offer task choices; use a break card system; reinforce task initiation and completion with short earned breaks; teach John to request help appropriately.

A — Antecedent

Therapist prompts Maria to transition from playing with blocks to a puzzle activity.

B — Behavior

Maria bangs her head lightly against the wall and cries.

C — Consequence

Therapist stops the puzzle activity and comforts Maria.

Hypothesis Statement: When prompted to transition away from preferred activities (blocks), Maria engages in self-injurious behavior (head-banging, crying) in order to avoid transitions and maintain access to preferred items.

Intervention Idea: Use visual schedules with picture-based transition warnings; provide a 2-minute countdown before transitions; pair new activity with preferred items; reinforce successful transitions immediately.

A — Antecedent

Parents are talking to each other and not paying attention to Alex during mealtime.

B — Behavior

Alex throws food on the floor and shouts.

C — Consequence

Parents immediately scold Alex and focus full attention on him.

Hypothesis Statement: When parents are not attending to Alex (mealtime inattention), Alex engages in disruptive behavior (throwing food, shouting) in order to gain immediate adult attention.

Intervention Idea: Teach Alex appropriate attention-requesting behavior; reinforce appropriate requests with immediate attention; minimize parental attention to disruptive acts (planned ignoring); schedule 1:1 family time proactively.

A — Antecedent

Teacher gives Liam finger paints and asks him to create a picture.

B — Behavior

Liam screams, pushes the paint away, and covers his hands.

C — Consequence

Teacher allows Liam to use crayons instead.

Hypothesis Statement: When presented with tactile/messy sensory materials (finger paint), Liam engages in avoidance behavior (screaming, pushing materials away) in order to escape uncomfortable tactile stimulation.

Intervention Idea: Implement a gradual sensory desensitization plan; begin with less-messy tools and introduce finger paint incrementally; pair sensory activities with preferred items; consult occupational therapist for sensory integration strategies.
